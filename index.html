PWAA · Strategic Framework · May 2026
Built from the evidence of the Women Deliver talanoa, this framework establishes PWAA as a national development organisation for Pacific women in Australia. Not a replication of what state and city-based Pacific women's organisations already do, but the infrastructure layer they cannot build themselves: a membership body with a constituency, nationally coordinated capacity building, train-the-trainer delivery, thematic working groups driving policy advocacy, Pacific women-led research, and a living intelligence database of Pacific women leaders and organisations across Australia.

01 · Strategic Positioning
What this is — and what it is not
The risk with any new PWAA is that it competes with what already exists, or creates another layer of coordination without adding real value. This framework is designed to avoid both.

"We are not building a peak body. We are building the infrastructure that helps every Pacific women's organisation in Australia do its work better — and ensures the women within those organisations can do theirs."

What already exists
State-based and city-level Pacific women's organisations doing community services, cultural programmes, advocacy, and social support. PACIFICA, MWWL, and others across the country. They know their communities. They are embedded.
What the gap is
No national body with a specific focus on capability uplift across the seven advocacy themes. No policy advocacy arm connecting Pacific women's lived experience to law and policy reform. No research infrastructure generating Pacific women-led evidence. No train-the-trainer pipeline. No shared intelligence database of who is doing what, where.
What PWAA fills
A national platform that serves existing organisations rather than competing with them. Delivers specialist capacity building they cannot build themselves. Houses a policy advocacy arm that translates Pacific women's experience into evidence-based submissions, reform agendas, and legislative engagement. Generates and commissions research that Pacific women own. Creates the connective tissue between Pacific women's expertise and the policy and sector spaces that need it.
Design principle: Every programme and resource PWAA creates should be deliverable by a state-based organisation to their own community. The PWAA trains the trainers — it does not own the relationship with community.

The Women Deliver talanoa produced a clear picture: Pacific women in Australia are highly capable across all seven themes, they want to take action, and they have identified specific things that are blocking them. The most consistent blockers are capacity (skills in report writing, policy engagement, civic navigation), visibility (no shared map of who the leaders are), coordination (no mechanism for Pacific women to work together nationally across themes), and evidence (no Pacific women-owned research base to support advocacy). PWAA addresses all four — through its capacity building programmes, intelligence database, policy advocacy arm, and research function working as an integrated system.

02 · Identity
Pacific Women's Association Australia
The name is confirmed. The rationale below explains the thinking — useful for articulating to funders, partners, and community why this name was chosen.

Why "Association": Signals membership, legitimacy, and permanence to funders and institutions. Broad enough to encompass capability building, advocacy, network, and database functions without over-specifying. Familiar enough to not require explanation. Does not claim to be a "peak body" — which would invite challenge from existing state-based organisations.

Why "Pacific Women's" not "Pasifika Women's": "Pacific" is the more legible term to non-Pacific audiences, government, and funders — which matters for an organisation operating at a national institutional level. "Pasifika" can be used in programme names, community communications, and cultural contexts where community recognition is the priority. The two work together rather than competing.

Why "Australia" at the end: Places the geographic mandate clearly while leaving room for cross-regional work with Pacific women's networks in New Zealand and the broader Pacific. "Australian Pacific Women's Association" would have front-loaded the geography and weakened the Pacific identity. "Australia" as a suffix reads as jurisdiction, not identity.

Pacific language name: PWAA should carry a Pacific language name alongside the English name — to be determined by the founding collective through talanoa. This is not decoration: the Pacific language name is how PWAA identifies itself to community. The English name is how it operates in institutional spaces. Both are the real name.
03 · Programme Pillars
Seven themes. Seven capacity streams.
The Women Deliver talanoa identified seven advocacy themes through the Head/Heart/Feet framework. Each becomes a dedicated capacity building stream. PWAA does not try to do everything — it builds the capability of Pacific women to lead within each theme themselves.
01
Economic Justice
Priority stream
What the talanoa showed
  • Cost of living crisis falling hardest on Pacific women and families
  • Gender pay gap compounded for Pacific and Māori women
  • Remittances as economic lifeline — not recognised in policy
  • Informal sector work invisible in economic measurement
  • Knowledge gap on global economic systems — intergenerational
Capacity building focus
  • Financial literacy and economic advocacy training
  • Writing economic policy submissions and budget responses
  • Engaging parliamentary processes around pay equity
  • Train the trainer: economic literacy facilitation for community
  • Connecting diaspora economic networks across Australia and the Pacific
02
Climate
Priority stream
What the talanoa showed
  • Pacific women are frontline climate advocates — expertise already exists
  • Diaspora disconnected from on-island climate realities
  • Gap between community knowledge and policy engagement skills
  • Need to connect to international climate mechanisms (COP, UNFCCC)
Capacity building focus
  • Climate policy literacy and advocacy pathway training
  • Preparing for and participating in international climate forums
  • Community climate storytelling for policy and media audiences
  • Train the trainer: climate justice facilitation for Pacific communities
  • Connecting diaspora advocates to on-island women leaders
03
Gender Equity
Foundational stream
What the talanoa showed
  • Gendered violence compounded by economic stress
  • Culturally complex navigation of gender norms within community
  • Pacific women under-represented in gender equity policy spaces
  • CEDAW and UPR mechanisms unknown to most community advocates
Capacity building focus
  • Understanding and using CEDAW and UPR reporting mechanisms
  • Pacific-centred gender equity framing — not imported frameworks
  • Engaging DV and gender safety policy processes
  • Train the trainer: gender equity facilitation grounded in Pacific values
04
Peace & Security
Priority stream
What the talanoa showed
  • Militarisation in the Pacific region a live concern for diaspora women
  • West Papua, Kanaky, Ma'ohi Nui — self-determination struggles
  • Pacific women's voices absent from peace and security policy
  • Need for cross-Pacific women's solidarity on security issues
Capacity building focus
  • Women, Peace and Security (UNSCR 1325) framework training
  • Writing UPR reports documenting militarisation impacts
  • Mapping and engaging parliamentarians on Pacific security issues
  • Train the trainer: peace advocacy facilitation for Pacific communities
05
Health
Active stream
What the talanoa showed
  • Health disparities for Pacific women compounded by cost of living
  • Mental health impacts of diaspora experience under-resourced
  • Community health navigation skills unevenly distributed
  • Pacific women as informal health navigators for families
Capacity building focus
  • Health literacy and health system navigation training
  • Engaging health policy processes with Pacific women's data
  • Mental health first aid — Pacific cultural framing
  • Train the trainer: health advocacy facilitation for community
06
Leadership
Priority stream
What the talanoa showed
  • Pacific has one of world's lowest rates of women's political participation
  • No pipeline: no training, no support, no preparation pathways
  • Political environments actively hostile to Pacific women
  • No legislated quotas; some communities actively exclude women from leadership
  • Civics knowledge gap — women don't know how to navigate political systems
Capacity building focus
  • Civics education — how Australian democratic systems actually work
  • Pathways to Parliament programme — tailored for Pacific women
  • Board and governance readiness training
  • Train the trainer: leadership facilitation and mentoring frameworks
  • Connecting Pacific women to political networks and sponsors
07
Cultural Arts
Emerging stream
What the talanoa showed
  • Cultural arts as advocacy — underutilised and underfunded
  • Pacific artists lack access to funding mechanisms and sector networks
  • Cultural preservation work invisible in economic and policy measurement
  • Arts and culture as entry point for Pacific women who don't identify with "advocacy"
Capacity building focus
  • Arts grant writing and funding navigation for Pacific artists
  • Using cultural practice as policy advocacy — training and frameworks
  • IP and cultural rights literacy for Pacific artists and communities
  • Train the trainer: cultural arts facilitation and community programme design
04 · Delivery Model
How it works in practice
The delivery model has three distinct and mutually reinforcing components. Each is designed to build the others over time.
A
Capacity Building Programmes
Short, intensive programmes (half-day to two-day) in each of the seven theme areas. Designed for Pacific women who want to take action but need specific skills and knowledge to do so. Delivered nationally — rotating cities, online cohorts, and intensive retreats.
B
Train the Trainer Pathway
Each programme has a trainer certification track. Pacific women who complete the programme can go on to become certified facilitators who deliver it inside their own state-based organisations and communities. PWAA trains them; they own the local delivery.
C
Intelligence Database
A living, maintained database of Pacific women leaders, advocates, and organisations across Australia. Mapped by geography, sector, theme expertise, and community language. Used for referral, connection, collaboration, and funding intelligence.
D
Policy Advocacy Arm
A dedicated function within PWAA that translates Pacific women's collective knowledge and lived experience into formal policy engagement. Prepares submissions to government inquiries, budget processes, and legislative reviews across the seven theme areas. Coordinates Pacific women's voices into unified policy positions rather than fragmented individual responses. Directly connected to the research function — evidence informs advocacy, advocacy identifies research priorities. This is what gives PWAA national credibility and relevance beyond community delivery.
E
Research & Evidence
PWAA generates, commissions, and synthesises research on Pacific women's experience in Australia across the seven themes. Research is Pacific women-led and community-owned — not research done on Pacific women, but research produced by them. Findings feed directly into the policy advocacy arm and into programme design. Dr Rita Seumanutafa-Palala (Oceania Institute) provides the academic anchor for this function. Over time, PWAA becomes the primary evidence base that governments and funders turn to when they need to understand Pacific women's experience in Australia.
Key design rule for train-the-trainer: Trainers are certified to deliver the programme as designed, and are also supported to adapt it for their community context. Pacific communities are not monolithic. A Health advocacy session delivered in Western Sydney to a Samoan community will be different in tone and example from the same session delivered in Brisbane to a Fijian-Indian community. The framework travels; the delivery is contextual.
Programme design principles
  • All programmes grounded in Pacific knowledge frameworks — talanoa, va, fa'afaletui
  • Pacific women as experts, not recipients — build on what they already know
  • No "deficit" framing — programmes build on existing capability
  • Every programme produces something usable: a report, a submission, a plan
  • Online and in-person options — accessibility is non-negotiable
  • Peer cohort model — participants build network as they build skills
  • Sliding scale or fully subsidised — cost must not be the barrier
Train the trainer requirements
  • Complete the core programme in the relevant theme area
  • Complete a facilitation skills module (Pacific-centred pedagogy)
  • Co-facilitate one delivery under supervision
  • Annual renewal — certified trainers stay connected to the PWAA
  • Trainers receive a small delivery fee or resource support when they run locally
  • Trainers contribute feedback to improve programme quality over time
  • Community of practice — certified trainers meet quarterly nationally

Structure that serves, not controls
The governance model must reflect Pacific values — relational, accountable to community, and flat enough to move. It should not reproduce the bureaucratic structures that Pacific women are already navigating in other institutions.

07 · Intelligence Database
A living map of Pacific women's expertise in Australia
The database is not a directory. It is a strategic intelligence tool — for connection, referral, funder navigation, and policy engagement. It answers the question that every Pacific woman has had to ask at some point: "Who else is doing this work?"

"The room at Women Deliver held 40+ organisations and none of them had a shared map of each other. That is the gap the database fills — not just for this network, but for funders, governments, and institutions that claim to want to engage Pacific women and don't know where to find them."

New · Membership Model
Membership is the mandate
Every comparable national women's organisation — AMWA, NATSIWA, WWDA, the National Rural Women's Coalition — has a formal membership structure. Without one, PWAA has a database but no constituency. Membership is what gives PWAA the right to speak on behalf of Pacific women to government and funders. It is also a revenue stream, an accountability mechanism, and the structure that makes the database active rather than passive.

Design principle: A person listed in the PWAA database is a PWAA member. An organisation listed is an organisational member. The tiered database access model is also the membership tier model — they are the same architecture, named properly.

Individual
Community Member
Free — open to all Pacific women in Australia
  • Listed in the PWAA database (name, state, theme expertise)
  • Access to PWAA newsletter and policy updates
  • Eligible to attend PWAA capacity building programmes (subsidised rate)
  • Invitation to annual national gathering
  • Voice in PWAA's community consultations
Individual
Practitioner Member
$50–100/year — sliding scale
  • Full database profile with sector, languages, speaker availability
  • Full access to member database for connection and referral
  • Eligible for train-the-trainer certification pathway
  • Participation in thematic working groups
  • Priority access to PWAA programmes and events
  • Voting rights in PWAA governance elections
Organisational
Partner Organisation
$200–500/year — based on org size
  • Organisation listed in PWAA database with full profile
  • Up to 5 staff listed as Practitioner Members
  • Host delivery rights — PWAA programmes delivered in your community
  • Named in PWAA partnership communications and reports
  • Seat at annual national gathering
  • Input into PWAA policy positions affecting your community
On the relationship to existing Pacific women's organisations: State-based Pacific women's organisations — PACIFICA, MWWL, and others — are invited to join as Partner Organisations. This formalises the relationship: PWAA trains their members, hosts delivery in their communities, and names them in all partnership communications. They are not competitors. They are the delivery network that makes PWAA's national reach real.
New · Policy Mechanism
Thematic Working Groups
The policy advocacy arm needs a mechanism, not just a mandate. Each of PWAA's seven programme pillars has a corresponding thematic working group — a standing body of Pacific women with expertise in that theme, convened by PWAA, producing annual policy positions and coordinating responses to government inquiries. This is the structure that transforms individual Pacific women's expertise into a unified, credible national advocacy voice.

AMWA operates thematic working groups for each of its key focus areas. PWAA should build this structure from the start — not retrofit it later. Each working group simultaneously serves as the policy mechanism, a member engagement pathway, and a community of practice for certified trainers in that theme.

01 · Economic Justice Working Group
Produces annual economic policy position. Coordinates submissions to budget processes, pay equity inquiries, and cost of living reviews. Connects diaspora economic networks. Monitors remittance policy.
02 · Climate Working Group
Coordinates Pacific women's voice in Australian climate policy. Prepares submissions to DCCEEW inquiries and climate legislation reviews. Connects diaspora advocates to on-island Pacific women's climate movements. Monitors COP engagement.
03 · Gender Equity Working Group
Produces Pacific women's CEDAW shadow report contributions. Coordinates submissions to DV, gender safety, and reproductive rights inquiries. Develops Pacific-centred gender equity frameworks for institutional use.
04 · Peace & Security Working Group
Coordinates Pacific women's voice on Australia's Women, Peace and Security National Action Plan. Produces UPR report contributions on militarisation impacts. Maps and engages parliamentarians on Pacific security issues.
05 · Health Working Group
Monitors health policy affecting Pacific women. Coordinates submissions to health system reviews, mental health inquiries, and culturally appropriate care standards. Partners with the Multicultural Centre for Women's Health.
06 · Leadership Working Group
Coordinates Pacific women's Pathways to Parliament advocacy. Produces submissions to electoral reform and governance diversity inquiries. Maps and mentors Pacific women candidates. Monitors political participation data.
07 · Cultural Arts Working Group
Coordinates Pacific artists' voice in arts funding policy. Produces submissions to Creative Australia and state arts bodies on Pacific arts representation. Develops IP and cultural rights frameworks for Pacific artists. Partners with PICAA and Oceania Institute.
Working group structure: Each group is convened by a Practitioner Member with demonstrated expertise in the theme. Groups meet quarterly online and in person at the annual gathering. Each group produces one annual policy position paper and coordinates up to three government submissions per year. Certified trainers in the relevant theme are automatically invited into the working group — creating the link between capacity building and advocacy.

New · Strategic Relationships
Partners to build from Phase 1
PWAA's credibility and reach will be built substantially through who it is formally aligned with. The following partnerships are not aspirational — they are strategic necessities that should be initiated in Phase 1, not Phase 3.
Phase 1–2 Horizon
AMWA Partner
PWAA becomes the recognised Pacific women's expert voice that AMWA draws on for government engagement on Pacific-specific issues. Formal partnership agreement. Seat at AMWA's Pacific consultations.
Phase 2–3 Horizon
National Women's Alliance Affiliate
PWAA is funded by Office for Women as a specialist Pacific women's voice — equivalent to how NATSIWA operates as the specialist First Nations women's voice within the broader NWA structure.
Phase 4+ Horizon
Pacific National Women's Alliance
PWAA achieves status as a standalone Pacific-specific National Women's Alliance — the equivalent of NATSIWA for Pacific women. Government-funded, community-owned, nationally recognised.
Australian Multicultural Women's Alliance (AMWA)
Strategic Alliance — initiate Phase 1
AMWA is the funded national voice for CALD women, led by FECCA. Pacific women sit within its mandate but are not centred. PWAA should approach AMWA for a formal partnership agreement — PWAA provides Pacific expertise and community connection; AMWA provides access to government policy processes and the National Women's Alliances framework. This is not competition: it is specialisation within a shared ecosystem.
National Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Women's Alliance (NATSIWA)
Peer Partnership — initiate Phase 1
Pacific women in Australia live and work on Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Country. PWAA needs a clear, substantive position on that relationship — not an acknowledgement formula. NATSIWA is the peer organisation that models what a First Nations-specific women's national body looks like within the NWA structure. A formal relationship with NATSIWA also signals to government that PWAA understands the broader sovereignty and rights landscape it operates within.
Department of Foreign Affairs and Trade (DFAT)
Funding Relationship — initiate Phase 2
Australia's Pacific foreign policy and DFAT's substantial Pacific budget make PWAA a legitimate funding target. PWAA's cross-regional work — connecting Australian diaspora women to Pacific region advocacy on climate, peace, and economic justice — is directly relevant to DFAT's Pacific priorities. Kavita Naidu's international human rights and climate profile is the access point. This should be named as a Phase 2 funding target in the roadmap.
Office for Women (PM&C) and Department of Home Affairs
Government Engagement — initiate Phase 2
Two distinct funding and engagement pathways. Office for Women funds the National Women's Alliances program ($10.88 million, December 2024 to June 2029) — PWAA should be building toward eligibility for the next round. Home Affairs administers the Multicultural Peak Body Funding program (up to $400k/year for four years for incorporated multicultural peak bodies) — PWAA should apply once incorporated with a demonstrated track record.
Multicultural Centre for Women's Health (MCWH)
Programme Partnership — initiate Phase 1
MCWH is the national specialist body on health for women from culturally diverse backgrounds. Partnership with MCWH strengthens PWAA's Health working group, provides access to existing health policy networks, and avoids duplication in the health space. Pacific women's specific health needs can be articulated within MCWH's existing government relationships.
Pacific Island Forum / Pacific regional women's networks
Cross-Regional Alliance — initiate Phase 2
PWAA's mandate includes connecting Australian diaspora women to Pacific regional advocacy movements. Formal relationships with Pacific regional women's networks — through the Pacific Island Forum, UN Women Pacific, and bilateral connections through Kavita Naidu and Jacynta Faamau — position PWAA as the bridge organisation between the diaspora and the region. This is the basis for the DFAT funding case and the long-term international credibility of the organisation.
New · Monitoring & Evaluation
How PWAA knows it is working
The roadmap has output milestones — numbers of women through programmes, database entries, trainer cohorts. These are necessary but not sufficient. Every funder and government body will ask what has changed for Pacific women in Australia as a result of PWAA existing. This outcomes framework answers that question at three levels.
Level 1 — Outputs
What PWAA delivers
  • Number of women through capacity building programmes
  • Number of trainers certified per theme
  • Number of community programme deliveries by certified trainers
  • Database entries — individuals and organisations
  • Policy submissions lodged per year
  • Members — individual and organisational
  • Working group sessions convened
Level 2 — Outcomes
What changes for Pacific women
  • Increased confidence and capability in policy engagement (pre/post programme survey)
  • Pacific women entering or advancing in leadership roles
  • Pacific women successfully lodging policy submissions
  • New connections formed between Pacific women across states and themes
  • Pacific organisations accessing funding they previously could not access
  • Certified trainers actively delivering in their communities
Level 3 — Impact
What changes in the system
  • Pacific women's voices represented in government policy processes
  • Pacific women-led evidence cited in policy documents and decisions
  • Reduction in structural barriers Pacific women identify as blockers
  • PWAA recognised as the authoritative voice on Pacific women's experience in Australia
  • Pacific women's issues named in national gender equality strategies
  • Pacific women's political participation rates improving over time

On research as evaluation: PWAA's research function is not separate from its monitoring and evaluation — it is the same work. The research arm generates the baseline data (what does Pacific women's experience in Australia look like now?) and tracks change over time (what is different because PWAA exists?). Dr Rita Seumanutafa-Palala's academic anchor role at Oceania Institute is critical here — PWAA's impact data should be publishable, citable, and able to stand up to government scrutiny.

Evaluation cadence: Annual output report to members and funders. Biennial outcomes report drawing on programme evaluations, working group assessments, and member surveys. Five-year impact evaluation — externally reviewed — to assess systemic change. The first five-year evaluation would be due in 2031, which aligns with the review of Australia's Women, Peace and Security National Action Plan and provides a natural policy moment to demonstrate PWAA's contribution.
